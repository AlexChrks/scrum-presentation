Scrum - agile project management framework

Let's consider Artifacts in scrum:
Product backlog
  • A list of all desired work on the project
  • All requirements are described according to a single template, which is called User Story
  • The requirements are designed in such a way that it is obvious and understandable what value they represent to the user.
  • Prioritized by the product owner
  • Reprioritized at the start of each sprint
Sprint backlog:
  • On this slide you can note difference between Product backlog and sprint Backlog
  • Product backlog act like a parent for Sprint Backlog
  • Obviously, Sprint Backlog is a list of all the work that needs to be done in the next sprint
  • During the sprint, new requirements should not appear in the Sprint backlog.
  • All requirements must be divided into tasks and evaluated
Sprint burndown chart
  • On this slide represented a sprint burndown chart.
  • Person-hours or ideal units (or Story Points) act as "burning" elements.
  • The diagram is updated every time a task is completed.
  • In practice, this diagram is very clear: every day you can see how much the team has progressed.
Let's consider roles in scrum.
  • Product owner is the person who:
  • Formulates requirements
  • Prioritizes them according market requirements
  • Corrects requirements for each sprint
  • Also he is personally responsible for the value of the requirements for the market / users
  • Only one person
  • The Product Owner is the representative of the unit that owns the product being developed.
  • Monitors the correct using of Agile principles and Scrum processes
  • Organizes the work of the team and provides team with everything necessary
  • Protects the team, is responsible for its effectiveness
  • Only one person.
  • The Scrum Master does not assign people to tasks - this is done by the team itself;
  • The Master does not force people to do the work - it is the responsibility of the team;
  • The Master does not tell the Product Owner what requirements he should write - this is the work of the product owner.
  • The Scrum Master's functions are much broader, but a separate presentation is needed to explain them all.
  • Typically team consist of from 5 to 9 people
  • Cross-functional: Programmers, testers, user experience designers, etc.
  • Members should be full-time. May be exceptions (e.g., database administrator)
  • Teams are self-organizing
  • Membership should change only between sprints
    • The team is responsible for product development in iterations (sprints). The team determines independently:
    • sprint duration
    • command capacity
    • the size of its focus factor
    • the complexity of the requirements that will be implemented in the sprint
    • priority of tasks and much more
Now let's consider ceremonies also named as rituals in scrum
  • Sprint Planning answers the following questions:
  • What can be delivered in the Increment resulting from the upcoming Sprint?
  • How will the work needed to deliver the Increment be achieved?
  • Work is selected from the Product Backlog and pulled into the Sprint Backlog.
  • The work in the Sprint Backlog is not a commitment, it's a forecast.
  • Daily standups, also known as Daily Scrum, are quick daily Scrum meetings that the Scrum team has every day of the sprint.
  • The meetings should be time-boxed to 15 minutes to keep the discussion quick but effective.
  • Daily Scrum meetings should be held at the same time and same place every day. It’s best to have them in the morning before the Scrum team starts their work.
  • Daily standups should be attended by the whole team - Scrum Master, product owner/manager and developers.
    • Each team member should answer following questions:
    • What they did the previous day
    • What they’re planning to do that day
    • Whether there are any obstacles in their way
  • Sprint review is the meeting at the end of the sprint where the Scrum team and all the stakeholders get together and discuss what has been accomplished during the sprint and whether the sprint goal has been met.
  • The meeting should be attended by the product owner and manager, Scrum Master, development team, management, and anyone else who is involved in the product creation process.
  • This is usually an informal type of meeting where the Scrum team presents the product increment, while the other stakeholders provide feedback.
  • Sprint review meetings should have a few things on the agenda:
    • The product owner needs to inform the team on the tasks that have been completed and those that are still not done.
    • The developers need to discuss the problems they encountered during the sprint and how they were solved.
    • The Scrum team answers sprint-related questions from the rest of the team.
    • The team should discuss which tasks come next, something that gives a great basis for the next sprint planning meeting.
